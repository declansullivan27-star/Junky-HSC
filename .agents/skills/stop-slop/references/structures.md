# Structural patterns to avoid

These are layout and formatting habits that pad length without adding meaning.

## The echo opener
Restating the question or request before answering it.
> "You asked about X. X is an important topic. Here's what I know about X."
→ Just answer. The person knows what they asked.

## The affirmation sandwich
Opening with praise, then giving the real answer, then closing with an offer to help more.
> "Great question! [answer] Let me know if you need anything else!"
→ Just give the answer.

## Bullet-point everything
Turning continuous thought into bullets because bullets look organized, not because the content is actually a list.
→ If the ideas connect, write sentences. Use bullets only for genuinely enumerable, parallel items.

## The identical bullet opener
Every bullet starts with the same part of speech or the same word.
> - Ensure that...
> - Ensure that...
> - Ensure that...
→ Vary sentence structure. If they all say the same verb, the structure is hiding repetition.

## The fake three-part structure
Padding a single point into "First... Second... Finally..." when there's really one idea.
→ One idea = one sentence or one paragraph. Don't manufacture structure.

## The disclaimer wall
Adding caveats, limitations, and "this may vary" hedges before giving any useful information.
→ Give the answer. Add a caveat only if it genuinely changes what someone would do.

## The context dump
Explaining background the reader already knows before getting to what they actually need.
> "Kansas City is a city in Missouri. Junk removal is the process of..."
→ Start where the reader is, not at zero.

## The summary that repeats the body
Ending a piece with a "Summary" or "Conclusion" section that just re-lists what was in the body.
→ If the piece needs a summary to make sense, the body isn't clear enough. Fix the body.

## Orphan headers
Adding bold headers or section titles to responses so short they don't need navigation.
→ Headers are for documents people will scan. A 3-paragraph reply doesn't need headers.

## The passive voice default
Using passive voice to avoid saying who did what.
> "Mistakes were made." / "The file was not found."
→ Say who or what: "The deploy failed." / "We made a mistake."

## Symmetry padding
Making every list item the same length, or every section the same word count, by adding filler to the short ones.
→ Let items be as long as they need to be.

## The hedge ladder
Stacking multiple hedges on one statement until it means nothing.
> "It's possible that this might potentially have some effect in certain cases."
→ Commit to a claim or don't make it.
